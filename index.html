AN ACT PUNISHING ONLINE SEXUAL ABUSE OF EXPLOITATION OF CHILDREN, PENALIZING THE PRODUCTION, DISTRIBUTION, POSSESSION AND ACCESS OF CHILD SEXUAL ABUSE OR EXPLOITATION MATERIALS, AMENDING REPUBLIC ACT NO. 9160, OTHERWISE KNOWN AS THE “ANTI-MONEY LAUNDERING ACT OF 2001”, AS AMENDED AND REPEALING REPUBLIC ACT NO. 977, OTHERWISE KNOWN AS THE “ANTI-CHILD PORNOGRAPHY ACT OF 2009”

[Enacted on 2022]

Legislative Policy and Purpose

The State recognizes the vital role of the youth in nation-building and shall protect their physical, moral, spiritual, intellectual, emotional, psychological, and social well-being.


RA 11930 provides special protection to children from all forms of sexual violence, abuse, and exploitation committed through ICT and other digital means.


The law also seeks to guarantee children's rights, comply with international treaties, and ensure safe access to digital technologies.

Important Definitions

Child: A person below 18 years old, including persons portrayed as children and computer-generated representations.


OSAEC: Use of ICT to sexually abuse or exploit children.


CSAEM/CSAM: Any material showing a child engaged in real or simulated sexual activity.

Acts Punished and Corresponding Penalties

Section Punishable Act Penalty
Sec. 4 (a)-(j) Producing, distributing, facilitating, streaming, trafficking, or exploiting children for CSAEM or OSAEC activities. Life imprisonment + fine not less than ₱2,000,000
Sec. 4 (k)-(l) Knowingly benefiting from or providing venues for prohibited acts. Reclusion temporal maximum to reclusion perpetua + ₱1,000,000–₱2,000,000 fine
Sec. 4 (m)-(o) Grooming, sexualizing children online, and pandering. Reclusion temporal maximum + ₱800,000–₱1,000,000 fine
Sec. 4 (p) Supporting or subscribing to websites hosting CSAEM or OSAEC content. Reclusion temporal medium + ₱500,000–₱800,000 fine
Sec. 4 (q) Advertising or promoting OSAEC and CSAEM materials. Reclusion temporal minimum + ₱300,000–₱500,000 fine
Sec. 4 (r) Possession of CSAEM materials. Reclusion temporal + fine not less than ₱300,000
Sec. 4 (s) Willful access to CSAEM materials. Prision mayor maximum + ₱200,000–₱300,000 fine
Sec. 4 (t) Conspiracy to commit prohibited acts. Prision mayor medium + ₱100,000–₱200,000 fine
Sec. 6 Syndicated commission of offenses under RA 11930. Life imprisonment + ₱5,000,000–₱20,000,000 fine

Relevant Jurisprudence

People v. YYY, G.R. No. 262941 (February 20, 2024)


Facts: The FBI tracked online child exploitation activities connected to YYY. Philippine authorities conducted surveillance, secured a search warrant, rescued minors, and recovered explicit digital evidence involving a child victim.


Issue: Whether the repeal of RA 9775 by RA 11930 extinguished criminal liability.


Ruling: No. The Supreme Court ruled that RA 11930 reenacted and expanded the prohibited acts under the former law, therefore criminal liability continued despite the repeal.

Illustrative Application

An adult who emotionally manipulates a minor online and convinces them to send sexual videos may be liable for grooming and producing CSAEM.


Possession of three or more CSAEM files is prima facie evidence of intent to distribute or publish the materials.


The consent of the child is not a valid defense.

Recent Developments

The government launched a roadmap against online sexual abuse and exploitation of children focused on:

  • Prevention
  • Protection
  • Enforcement
  • International and local partnerships
